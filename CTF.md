# On CTF

CTF has become one of my favorite ways to actually learn this stuff. I've been competing through NCL/CyberSkyline across most of the categories: web, network forensics, privesc, memory forensics, crypto, RE, password cracking. Basically whatever they throw at me.

Anything involving a PCAP and Wireshark is where I feel the strongest. I know the tool well and I've got a solid networking foundation, so opening up a capture and pulling apart the traffic feels natural. There's a rhythm to it: filter, follow, narrow down, find the thing.

Reverse engineering and password cracking are the ones that wreck me. Both are categories where I've had to swallow my pride and read writeups more than once. But honestly that's part of why I keep showing up. I'd rather get humbled now in a CTF than later in a real environment.

I keep doing them because they're fun, mostly. Practice is a close second. Competition is way down the list. This is my first real run at CTFs and treating it like a competition instead of a learning experience would be silly. Right now my job is to get better, not to win.

## Receipts

Some time after writing the above, NCL put out the scouting reports for Spring 2026. Here's what the data says.

**Individual Game** — 726 out of 7,010 players (90th percentile). Played April 10–12.

**Team Game** — 124 out of 3,634 (97th percentile) with "The Log Hunters" out of SANS.edu. Played April 24–26.

Full breakdown and verification links in [ncl-spring-2026/](./ncl-spring-2026/).

A few things the numbers either confirmed or complicated:

The PCAP and Wireshark comment held up. Network Traffic Analysis was 300/300 in the team game. Scanning & Recon was perfect in both games. Log Analysis the same. The "filter, follow, narrow down, find the thing" categories are where I score, consistently.

Password cracking really is where I get wrecked. 74th percentile individual, my lowest score of the season. I got the two easy challenges (Hashed, Best64) and then four straight zeros on everything medium and up — Upload, Oph!, Unlocking, Serpens. Accuracy was 100% only because I never guessed; I just couldn't get to answers I could submit.

The team game number is the part I keep coming back to. Going from 90th to 97th percentile with teammates wasn't because I got better in two weeks. It was because teammates handled the categories I struggle with, which freed me up to spend time on the ones I'm good at. That's basically how SOC work runs, so the lesson is useful.
